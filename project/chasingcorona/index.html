Chasing Corona

2020

Last updated on Mar 5, 2020

Introduction

The recent (February 2020) outbreak of the COVID-19 corona virus has prompted an impressively rapid and joint response of epidemiologists, geneticists, software developers and data analysts. This has resulted in a number of insightful web-based dashboard tools to track and better understand the movements of this virus for specific countries and throughout the world1:

For instance, Johns Hopkins University (JHU) provides a dashboard showing counts of confirmed cases, deaths and recoveries for most countries and regions world-wide:

What struck me about these dashboards is that although they provide comprehensive reports on the absolute number of cases identified – some even provide in-depth information on individual cases(!) – they lack a population-wide context. In particular, I found myself wondering what these numbers meant in terms of the fraction of (per-country) populations affected.

Approach

I reasoned that perhaps a more interpretable picture of the scale and development of the situation could be provided by normalizing the number of confirmed COVID-19 cases per country with per-country population estimates.

Luckily the folks at JHU have freely made available their meticulously curated datasets through a GitHub repository, which they continue to update on a daily basis. In addition, the World Bank provides population count estimates for over 250 countries world-wide.

Combined2, these datasets open up an alternative view of the COVID-19 situation, in terms of the percentage of per-country populations (confirmed to be) affected by the virus.

DISCLAIMER: I am not a virologist or epidemiologist – I realize there are many caveats to interpreting these data, most of which I am not addressing here.
